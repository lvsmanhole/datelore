There is a particular cruelty in the way April 15 is arranged. The date that produced, in 1452, the most celebrated polymath in the Western canon — Leonardo da Vinci, painter, anatomist, engineer, cataloguer of everything the eye could reach — is also the date that, in 1912, watched one of the era's great engineering projects sink into the North Atlantic. The RMS Titanic went down at 2:20 in the morning, carrying 1,514 people with it. Only 710 of the 2,224 aboard survived. Four and a half centuries separate Leonardo's birth and the Titanic's end, but the calendar puts them on the same square, and it is hard not to read something in that proximity: here is what human ingenuity looks like in full flower, and here is what it looks like when confidence outruns care.

The date is not subtle about this pairing. On April 15, 1707, Leonhard Euler was born in Basel — the Swiss mathematician whose output was so enormous that journals were still publishing his unpublished papers decades after his death, who gave us the notation for functions, for the imaginary unit, for the base of the natural logarithm. The same April 15, in 1858, produced Émile Durkheim, who set about doing for society what Euler had done for number: finding the underlying structure, naming the forces invisible to the naked eye. Two architects of how we understand the world. And yet the date, in 1989, would stage the Hillsborough disaster — a human crush at Sheffield Wednesday's stadium during an FA Cup semi-final that killed 97 Liverpool fans. Not a failure of materials or mathematics, but a failure of crowd management and institutional judgment. Something that could have been seen, mapped, prevented.

Perhaps this is what the date is really about: the gap between what human minds can construct and what human institutions fail to protect.

### The weight Lincoln added

The gap runs through April 15, 1865, the morning Abraham Lincoln died after being shot the previous evening at Ford's Theatre. He had guided the Union through the war's full length; he did not survive to see the peace he had shaped. His death is the date's deepest shadow, the one that does not allow a merely ironic reading of the rest. And yet even here the pairing holds: Lincoln's death fell on a date that had, in 1947, sent Jackie Robinson onto the field for the Brooklyn Dodgers — his Major League debut, the moment he formally broke baseball's color line. One April 15 closes a chapter in the violence of American division; another opens one in its slow, contested undoing.

That same year the Titanic went down, 1912, a U.S. Senator named John B. Kendrick was not yet in office — but it was on April 15, 1922, that Kendrick introduced the Senate resolution that would unravel the Teapot Dome scandal, one of the era's great exposures of official corruption. Another kind of structure hidden beneath a respectable surface, another April 15 pulling it into the light.

### What the date keeps

The date is not all tragedy and exposure. On April 15, 1738, London heard the premiere of Handel's opera *Serse* — a comedy, in fact, one that opens with its king singing an aria of absurd tenderness to a plane tree. And in 1952 the Boeing B-52 Stratofortress made its first flight, a machine that would define the next half-century of strategic aviation. The date makes room for beauty and for the merely consequential alongside the catastrophic.

What it does not do is resolve the argument it keeps reopening. Leonardo da Vinci was born on April 15; the Titanic sank on April 15. The date holds both without blinking — the reach of the human imagination and the distance between that reach and the world it is asked to govern. That distance is not always a failure of intelligence. It is sometimes a failure of attention, or care, or the willingness to imagine what goes wrong. April 15 does not tell you which. It just keeps filing the evidence.
