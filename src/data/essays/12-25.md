The logic of the calendar is supposed to be neutral, but some dates develop a personality over centuries. December 25 is meant to be a fixed point — a day the world pauses, decorates, and turns inward. What makes it stranger, looked at straight, is how many endings have chosen it.

Two Decembers in quick succession made the point with unusual bluntness. On December 25, 1989, Nicolae Ceausescu and his wife Elena were condemned in a summary trial and executed — the Romanian Revolution finding its hinge-moment on the one day of the year most reliably associated with goodwill. Two years later almost to the hour, on December 25, 1991, Mikhail Gorbachev resigned as President of the Soviet Union, the union itself dissolving the following day. The Cold War's long architecture did not collapse on a Monday in March; it collapsed on Christmas. Whether the date was chosen deliberately, stumbled into, or simply arrived at after the machinery of collapse ran its own course, the effect is the same: the year's most ceremonial day became the calendar's shorthand for a superpower's last breath.

This is not the only time December 25 has found itself hosting a transfer of authority that would echo for centuries. In 1100, Baldwin of Boulogne was crowned the first King of Jerusalem in the Church of the Nativity in Bethlehem — the coronation set deliberately on the feast day, in the very church the feast commemorates. The symbolism was exact, almost too exact, the kind of stagecraft that medieval power understood as its native language. Nearly a millennium earlier, in 1046, Henry III was crowned Holy Roman Emperor by Pope Clement II on the same date. The day has been a stage for investiture as long as there have been crowns to confer.

The births that share it carry their own quiet irony. Isaac Newton arrived in 1642 — a mind that would spend its life describing the mechanical laws governing a universe that the day's theology held to be the object of divine interruption. He kept the date modestly. Humphrey Bogart was born on December 25, 1899, the century turning around him unannounced.

The date also takes. Charlie Chaplin died on December 25, 1977 — the comedian whose art had been built on the figure of a small man navigating a world too large and indifferent for comfort, gone on the day the world decorates itself with warmth. James Brown died on the same date in 2006, leaving behind a silence where there had been, for decades, one of the most insistent and physical presences American music produced.

Even before its associations hardened, December 25 carried the particular gravity of the year's edge. The Christmas truce of 1914 — when soldiers on the Western Front put down their weapons across an unofficial pause — is the date at its most hopeful: a moment when the machinery of catastrophe simply stopped, briefly, because the calendar suggested it might. The machinery resumed. But the pause had happened.

What the date keeps demonstrating is that a day powerful enough to halt ordinary business is powerful enough to concentrate everything else into it — the coronations, the executions, the resignations, the deaths of performers who had given their lives to making people feel something. The festival frame does not sanitize what falls inside it. It only makes the contrast sharper. Gorbachev did not resign into an ordinary Tuesday. He resigned into Christmas, and the world watched with its decorations still up.
