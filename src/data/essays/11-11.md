At the eleventh hour of the eleventh day of the eleventh month, 1918, the guns stopped. The order had been signed before dawn in a railroad car parked on a siding in the forest of Compiègne, where German envoys put their names to an armistice with the Allies, and at eleven the long noise of four years simply ended. Men who had lived inside artillery for so long that silence had become unimaginable now stood in the trenches and listened to it arrive. Birdsong, somewhere. The First World War was over.

It is one of the few endings the calendar lets you hear. The number itself seems to insist on meaning — eleven, eleven, eleven — as though the century wanted at least one date it could not misremember. And on that same morning the date did double duty, because an ending in the west was a beginning in the east. On November 11, 1918, Józef Piłsudski assumed supreme military power in Poland, the symbolic first day of a Polish independence that more than a century of partition had denied. One war's last hour was one nation's first. The same sunrise that closed the trenches opened a country.

But November 11 is not a date that lets you rest in its relief, because it keeps a darker anniversary on the same square. On November 11, 1923 — five years to the day after the armistice — Adolf Hitler was arrested in Munich for high treason, for his role in the Beer Hall Putsch. The man who would one day tear up the peace of Compiègne, who would haul a nation back into the catastrophe the armistice was meant to end, sat in custody on the fifth anniversary of the silence. The date that stopped the war already held, by then, the figure who would restart it. The seed was planted in the same soil as the peace.

The day's other entries keep a strange company with all this. Fyodor Dostoevsky, the novelist of suffering and conscience, was born on November 11, 1821, as if to supply the date its moral witness in advance. Søren Kierkegaard, who spent his life on dread and the leap of faith, died on this day in 1855. In 1880 the bushranger Ned Kelly was hanged at Melbourne Gaol; in 1940 the Royal Navy struck at Taranto, the first all-aircraft ship-to-ship attack in naval history, a glimpse of how the next war would be fought from the air. And in 2004 the date took Yasser Arafat, a man whose own long argument with peace never quite resolved.

What the armistice could not do was end the thing beneath the war. It silenced the guns; it did not silence the men who wanted them. November 11 keeps both truths in the same frame — the morning the noise stopped, and the morning, five years on, when the next noise was already taking shape in a Munich cell. The day asks to be remembered, and remembering, it turns out, was never the same as preventing.
