The feast of Saint Valentine has always asked us to believe the calendar is capable of warmth. The observance is old enough that no one agrees on the saint himself — the martyrology offers several Valentines, and the church's calendar on this date lists Cyril and Methodius alongside him, the scholar-missionaries who gave the Slavic world its alphabet. Love, literacy, witness: these are February 14's aspirations.

The date's actual record is less romantic.

In 1779, on the bay at Kealakekua on the Island of Hawaii, James Cook — cartographer, circumnavigator, the man who mapped more of the Pacific than anyone before him — was killed during a confrontation with Native Hawaiians. He had sailed into and out of that bay twice, and the second visit ended at the waterline. On the same February 14, in Georgia, the Battle of Kettle Creek was being fought as part of the American Revolutionary War, a skirmish that historians mark as one of the few Patriot victories in the Deep South that winter. The date held two violent confrontations simultaneously, on opposite sides of the world, as if it had decided to demonstrate its range.

That habit persists across the centuries. In 1939, the German battleship Bismarck was launched — a weapon that would spend its short career reshaping what naval warfare cost. In 1990, Indian Airlines Flight 605 crashed in Bangalore, killing ninety-two people. In 2004, the roof of the Transvaal water park in a suburb of Moscow collapsed, killing more than twenty-eight people and wounding nearly two hundred others. In 2008, a gunman opened fire in a lecture hall at Northern Illinois University in DeKalb County, Illinois, leaving five students dead and twenty-one wounded. The same year that saw YouTube's quiet launch — February 14, 2005, when a group of college students set the platform online — would eventually carry those events in searchable perpetuity.

The one entry that holds against all of this is a founding. On February 14, 1852, Great Ormond Street Hospital for Sick Children opened in London — the first hospital in England to provide in-patient beds exclusively for children. It is a small institutional fact, easy to pass over, but it is the date's one act of pure organized tenderness: a decision, made permanent in brick and endowment, that sick children deserved a place built specifically for them. Valentine's Day has always been framed as a private emotion. Great Ormond Street turned the same instinct into architecture, into wards, into clinical routine.

In 2011, Bahraini protesters gathered for what they called a Day of Rage, the opening act of the country's Arab Spring uprising. The date kept its pattern: rupture announced formally, hope and violence arriving together, the tender name on the calendar and the harder thing underneath.

The almanac does not choose what a date means. It records what the date has held — Cook dead at the shoreline and a children's hospital founded the same morning, seventy-three years apart; the Bismarck launched on the day lovers exchange letters; a gunman in a lecture hall on the feast of a saint whose story no one can fully verify. What February 14 accumulates, over centuries, is not romance. It is the uncomfortable proximity of care and catastrophe, the way the world refuses to pause its worst business for its most sentimental holiday. Great Ormond Street still stands. The Bismarck does not. The calendar holds both.
