Some dates collect a theme the way a low place collects water. November 9 collects the twentieth century's hardest question — what to do when something monstrous is gathering — and it keeps asking it in different voices.

Begin in Munich. On November 9, 1923, police and government troops crushed the Nazi Beer Hall Putsch, scattering an obscure agitator's first lunge at power. It looked, that morning, like an ending. Fifteen years later to the day — November 9, 1938 — the same movement, now the state, orchestrated Kristallnacht, the pogrom the Nazis excused with the killing of the German diplomat Ernst vom Rath. The date that had once watched the movement broken in the street now watched it, in power, break the windows of a people. The calendar offers no commentary. It simply lets the two mornings sit on the same square, and lets you notice that a thing crushed is not always a thing finished.

The date keeps its argument running through the lives it ends, too. On November 9, 1940, Neville Chamberlain died — the prime minister whose name became shorthand for the hope that catastrophe could be reasoned with, accommodated, sent home with a signed page. Thirty years on, November 9, 1970, took Charles de Gaulle, who had answered the same threat with the opposite instinct: refusal, exile, a radio voice insisting France had not finished fighting. Appeasement and defiance, the two replies to a gathering darkness, share a death-day. You can read that as coincidence. The almanac's habit is to let coincidence accumulate until it starts to feel like a question.

It is not all weight. November 9, 1934 gave the world Carl Sagan, who spent a career arguing for the cosmic perspective — the pale, humbling view from far enough out that human cruelty and human courage both shrink to a pixel, and grow, somehow, more precious for it. The same date sharpened a different kind of nerve in 1985, when a twenty-two-year-old Garry Kasparov out-thought Anatoly Karpov to become the youngest world chess champion; and in 1818 it produced Ivan Turgenev, who wrote about Russia's reformers and reactionaries with something like the unblinking patience the date itself seems to keep.

What November 9 finally argues is that history rarely settles anything on schedule. The putsch failed and came back; the appeaser and the resister both ran out of days; the cosmos kept its scale regardless. The date does not resolve its argument. It just refuses to drop it.
