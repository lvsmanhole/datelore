Exactly five hundred years separate two of the most total moments in human history, and both of them fell on May 29.

In 1453, Ottoman armies under Sultan Mehmed II captured Constantinople after a fifty-three-day siege, ending the Roman Empire — an institution that had, in one form or another, organized the Mediterranean world for more than two thousand years. The last Byzantine emperor, Constantine XI Palaiologos, died in the fighting. What collapsed on that Tuesday was not merely a city or a dynasty but a continuous thread of political authority stretching back through the Caesars to the Republic, a thread so long that most people living under it had never imagined it could end. It could. It did. The date absorbed the fact without ceremony.

Five centuries later to the day, on May 29, 1953, Edmund Hillary and the Sherpa Tenzing Norgay became the first people to stand on the summit of Mount Everest. The ascent happened on Norgay's adopted birthday — he was thirty-nine. They reached the highest point on Earth and then came back down. No civilization ended. No thread snapped. Instead, a line was crossed that human beings had been moving toward for as long as there had been mountaineers to look up at the Himalayas and wonder if the top was reachable. The answer, it turned out, was yes.

Put these two events beside each other and something becomes visible about what a date can quietly hold. Constantinople represents the kind of threshold you fall through without choosing — the end arrived, and no amount of institutional weight or historical duration could stop it. Everest represents the kind of threshold you cross by will, by preparation, and by the capacity to keep moving upward when the air is thin enough to kill you. Both are extremes. Both are conclusions of a sort. They share a date, and the date does not editorialize about which kind of ending matters more.

May 29 is not only a day of vertical drama. In 1851, Sojourner Truth delivered her address at the Woman's Rights Convention in Akron, Ohio — the speech known as "Ain't I a Woman?" — a piece of oratory whose logic was itself a kind of threshold crossing, demanding that the category of womanhood include her, and through her, everyone who had been excluded from its protections. In 1932, World War I veterans began assembling in Washington, D.C. as the Bonus Army, men who had crossed one set of thresholds in service and now crossed the country to collect what they had been promised. In 1999, Olusegun Obasanjo took office as the first elected civilian head of state in Nigeria after sixteen years of military rule — another kind of passage, from one political era to another.

John F. Kennedy was born on this date in 1917. He would spend his presidency presiding over thresholds of his own, though the calendar of his life belongs to November.

What the date keeps insisting on, in its various registers, is the idea of the extreme point — the moment when something that was possible becomes actual, or when something that seemed permanent reveals it was not. The Roman Empire ended. The summit was reached. The speech was given; the veterans marched; the soldiers took Kaifeng in 1233; the oil flowed into the rivers at Norilsk in 2020. Each event is, in its own way, a report from the edge of something.

The five-hundred-year coincidence between Constantinople and Everest may be nothing but arithmetic. Or it may be the calendar's way of putting two definitions of a limit side by side — the limit that breaks you, and the limit you break through — and leaving it to you to decide which you are standing in front of.
