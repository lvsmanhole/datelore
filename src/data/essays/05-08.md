What a body can survive, and what it cannot, is the quiet through-line of May 8. Not in any grim, unified way — the date carries comedy and high politics alongside its extremities — but if you follow the thread, it keeps arriving at the same question: how far is it possible to go?

On May 8, 1978, Reinhold Messner and Peter Habeler reached the summit of Mount Everest without supplemental oxygen, a feat the medical establishment had widely considered lethal. The prevailing view held that above roughly 8,000 metres the human brain would simply fail — that no one could think, or move, or survive without bottled air in the so-called "death zone." Messner and Habeler did not disprove this by argument. They disproved it by arriving. The ascent took place in a state that Messner later described as something between dreaming and dying, each step a small negotiation with unconsciousness. They came back. The boundary of the possible moved.

The body endures differently when it has no choice. On May 8, 1950, peat cutters near Silkeborg, Denmark, uncovered what appeared to be a recently murdered man in a bog. The face was so well preserved — peaceful, almost — that they called the police. Tollund Man, as he came to be known, had been dead for approximately two thousand years. The cold acidity of the peat had done what no embalmer could: held him in near-perfect suspension, his expression legible across two millennia. His body had not survived in any living sense, but it had not disappeared either. It had simply waited, patient and strange, for someone to find him.

Julian of Norwich did not wait passively. On May 8, 1373, the English anchoress lay at what everyone around her believed was her deathbed, receiving a sequence of visions she would later describe in *Revelations of Divine Love* — the first book in the English language known to have been written by a woman. She survived, and spent years afterward asking herself what the visions meant. The work that came out of that asking is still read. Her most famous line — "all shall be well, and all shall be well, and all manner of thing shall be well" — was not the comfort of someone who had avoided suffering. It was the conclusion of someone who had looked directly at it and chosen a different answer.

The same week in 1933, Mohandas Gandhi began a twenty-one-day fast of self-purification, launching a campaign to support the Harijan movement — his term for those the caste system had placed at the bottom. Gandhi's fasts were neither performances nor suicides; they were a form of argument conducted entirely within his own body, a wager that voluntary suffering, made visible, could shift what political language could not.

Henry Dunant, born on this date in 1828, arrived at a similar conviction through different means. The Swiss businessman who co-founded the Red Cross had witnessed the Battle of Solferino in 1859 and could not process what he saw: tens of thousands of wounded soldiers left in the fields because no one was organized to help them. His response was to propose a neutral, international humanitarian organization — a structure for the care of bodies that transcended the wars that broke them. He shared the first Nobel Peace Prize in 1901.

May 8, 2025 brought its own threshold moment: the election of Cardinal Robert Francis Prevost as the 267th Pope, taking the name Leo XIV — the first American to hold the office, chosen by a conclave navigating an institution older than any of the borders in the room.

The date does not resolve neatly into a single lesson about endurance. Tollund Man and Messner's summit are not the same kind of extreme; Julian's visions and Gandhi's fast are not the same discipline. What the date holds, instead, is a recurring fascination with what happens when a human being presses right up against the limit of what is bearable or possible — and the surprising frequency with which something, or someone, comes through.
