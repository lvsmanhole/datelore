The coincidence at the center of July 4 feels at once too arranged to be accidental and too accidental to have been arranged. On the morning of July 4, 1826 — fifty years to the day from the adoption of the Declaration of Independence — John Adams died at his home in Quincy, Massachusetts. Hours later, in Monticello, Virginia, Thomas Jefferson died too. The second and third presidents of the United States, the primary author of the Declaration and one of its most forceful advocates, chose the republic's golden jubilee as their shared exit. Adams's reputed last words were that Jefferson still survived. He did not.

The coincidence so staggered their contemporaries that many took it as providential — a sign that the Founders had been marked for something. What it actually is, stripped of the supernatural gloss, is stranger: two men who had been bitter political enemies, then intimate correspondents in old age, who happened to outlive nearly everyone else from that first generation and then stopped on the same afternoon. History rarely produces arrangements this neat. When it does, they tend to calcify into myth before anyone can look at them plainly.

July 4 kept the habit. On the same date in 1831, James Monroe — fifth president, last of the Virginia dynasty — died, making him the third president to die on Independence Day in five years. The date had begun to feel less like a celebration and more like a reckoning: the generation that made the republic handing it back, one by one, through the calendar's most charged square.

What they were handing it back to was already, by 1863, being contested at a cost that July 4 also carries. On that date in 1863, the Confederate army at Vicksburg, Mississippi surrendered to Ulysses S. Grant after forty-seven days of siege. The fall of Vicksburg gave the Union control of the Mississippi River and effectively split the Confederacy in two; it happened, by accident or irony, on the birthday of the nation whose definition the war was deciding. The Declaration had promised something; Vicksburg was an argument about whether the promise applied to everyone.

Eighteen years later, on July 4, 1881, the Tuskegee Institute opened in Alabama, founded to offer Black Americans the education the post-war settlement had promised and the post-Reconstruction South had done everything to deny. That it opened on the Fourth is the sort of detail that invites reading, and probably should be read: an institution built for people the Declaration's authors largely failed to include in their original accounting, opening on the day that document is celebrated.

The date also gave the world Calvin Coolidge, born July 4, 1872 — the only American president born on Independence Day, a fact he was aware of and apparently found meaningful, though he expressed that meaning with his characteristic economy of words.

Marie Curie died on July 4, 1934, of aplastic anemia brought on by decades of radiation exposure — a death that is inseparable from the work that caused it, the research into radioactivity that had twice earned the Nobel Prize. The date took her the way it took the Founders: at the end of something irreplaceable.

The Founders' coincidence is the date's loudest note, but not its only one. July 4 is where the American story keeps returning to examine itself — the opening of Tuskegee, the fall of Vicksburg, the birth of the one president the day could legitimately claim. Each arrival and departure on this square asks roughly the same question Adams and Jefferson left unanswered when they stopped breathing within hours of each other: what, exactly, was declared, and for whom, and is it finished yet.
