Sigmund Freud did not believe in accidents. The slip of the tongue, the forgotten name, the coincidence that makes us shiver — for him these were never random but smuggled cargo, meaning the mind had hidden from itself and then arranged to deliver in disguise. It is a temptation, then, to do to his own birthday what he did to everyone else's dreams: to lay the date of May 6, 1856 on the couch and ask what it has been trying not to say.

The first thing the date confesses is an appetite for will. A century before Freud, on May 6, 1758, it produced Maximilien Robespierre, who would convince himself that virtue required terror and that he was the instrument of both — a man who read his own conscience as the voice of history and signed the executions accordingly. The date keeps choosing people who believe they are authoring themselves. Orson Welles arrived on May 6, 1915, and spent his genius turning a man's buried psyche into Citizen Kane, a film that does to a tycoon exactly what Freud did to his patients: digs beneath the public monument for the small wound that explains it. Tony Blair, born 1953, and George Clooney, born 1961, belong to the same family of the self-constructed, the persona managed like an estate.

A Freudian would note that the date does not only give. It takes, and what it takes is telling. On May 6, 1859, it claimed Alexander von Humboldt, who had tried to map the entire living world into one connected system, to prove that nothing in nature stands alone — an ambition not unlike the analyst's, the dream of a single hidden order beneath the visible chaos. On May 6, 1992, it took Marlene Dietrich, perhaps the most deliberately built screen persona of the century, a face arranged into legend and then guarded against the camera for her last decades. The same day, in 1910, took Edward VII, a king who had waited most of his life to become the role he was born for.

And then the detail Freud would have lingered over longest. On May 6, 1757, the English poet Christopher Smart was admitted to an asylum, beginning a six-year confinement — a mind judged too disordered to be free, locked away on the very date that would, a century later, deliver the man who insisted disorder was only meaning we had refused to hear.

None of this is true in the way Freud's patients were true. The date did not arrange itself; we arranged it, gathering these names because the pattern flatters us. But that was always his quietest point. The meaning is real even when we are the ones who made it.
