Every day on the calendar is, in some sense, an agreement — a collective decision to mark this rotation, name it, and let it accumulate meaning. February 29 is that agreement made visible. It arrives only when the math demands it, roughly once every four years, and yet when it does arrive, history fills it like any other day: with treaties and coups, with earthquakes and confessions, with people being born and people dying.

Gioachino Rossini was born on this day in 1792 — a man whose birthday the calendar withheld for most of his life. He handled this with the lightness it seemed to invite, reportedly joking that he had aged very slowly. History rewarded the joke by making him one of the most celebrated composers of his era. The date's scarcity did not diminish him. It just meant that his birthday, when it came, was a rarer occasion than most.

The events of February 29 do not match that lightness. In 1960, an earthquake of 5.7 magnitude struck Agadir, a coastal city in Morocco, with a perceived intensity rated as Extreme — the city was destroyed, and twelve thousand people were killed with another twelve thousand injured. The 1960 Agadir earthquake happened on a day that will not recur for another four years, but its weight is not measured in frequency. In 1996, Faucett Perú Flight 251 went down in the Andes, taking all 123 passengers and crew. Catastrophe observes no calendar logic.

There are the political upheavals too. On February 29, 2004, Jean-Bertrand Aristide was removed as president of Haiti in a coup — an ousting that carried the gravity of a nation's interrupted sovereignty. Six years later, on this same date in 2020, the United States and the Taliban signed the Doha Agreement, a framework aimed at ending nearly two decades of war in Afghanistan. Two leap-day events separated by sixteen years, both hinging on the question of who holds power and under what terms. The calendar offered no commentary; it simply let them share a square.

Also on February 29, 2008, Misha Defonseca admitted to fabricating her Holocaust memoir — a book in which she had claimed to have survived the war by living with wolves in European forests. The confession landed on a day that already struggles to be taken seriously, and yet the admission was entirely serious: a false testimony about one of history's most documented atrocities, unraveling on the rarest date of the year.

Luxembourg became the first country in the world to make all public transport free nationwide — also on February 29, 2020, the same leap day that saw the Doha Agreement signed. A single date holding both a war's formal cease-fire and a small nation's quiet experiment in civic generosity.

What February 29 teaches, finally, is that rarity is not the same as insignificance. The day asks only for the earth to complete its extra quarter-turn — and while it waits, life goes on accumulating, indifferent to the interval.
