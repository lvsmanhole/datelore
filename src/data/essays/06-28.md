The motorcade took a wrong turn onto Franz Joseph Street, the driver braked to reverse, and for a moment the open car sat nearly still in front of a delicatessen where a young Bosnian Serb happened to be standing. Two shots. Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria, whose death in 1914 anchors this date's ledger, slumped beside his wife, and within weeks the alliances of Europe folded shut like a trap. A single side street in Sarajevo became the hinge on which the entire century turned, and the wars, partitions, and ideologies that followed all trace some thread back to that stalled automobile.

The strange arithmetic of the calendar is that the Balkans returned to this same date to settle accounts. On June 28, 2001, Slobodan Milošević was extradited to the International Criminal Tribunal at The Hague to stand trial, the architect of the wars that tore Yugoslavia apart delivered to a courtroom rather than a battlefield. The symmetry is almost unbearable in its neatness: the region that struck the match in 1914 was, eighty-seven years later to the day, made to answer for one of the long catastrophe's final chapters. June 28 is sacred ground in Serbian memory, the anniversary of the medieval defeat at Kosovo, and both the assassins of 1914 and the nationalists of the 1990s reached for that mythology. The date that ignited and the date that judged were never accidents; they were chosen.

Ignition and resistance recur across the day like a refrain. In 1956, workers from the HCP factory in Poznań poured into the streets in one of the first major uprisings against communist rule anywhere in Europe, a demand for bread and dignity met with tanks. Six decades on, in 2016, the Atatürk Airport attack in Istanbul killed forty-two people, terror arriving at the threshold between continents that the Balkans have always guarded. And there is a gentler insurgency stitched into the date as well, for June 28 is International LGBTQ Pride Day, marking the anniversary of the Stonewall riots, a different uprising whose weapons were defiance and visibility rather than rifles.

It is fitting, then, that the date also belongs to thinkers who tried to imagine the social order anew. Jean-Jacques Rousseau, born in 1712, gave the modern world its grammar of the general will and the social contract, the very ideas later twisted by the nationalisms that bloodied his philosophical heirs. James Madison, who died on this date in 1836, spent his life engineering checks against exactly the concentrated power that Sarajevo's aftermath unleashed. One date can hold both the spark and the reckoning, the wound and the slow, imperfect labor of justice, and June 28 keeps them side by side without resolving the tension between them.
