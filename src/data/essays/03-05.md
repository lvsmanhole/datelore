They found him on the floor of the Volynskoe dacha outside Moscow, the most feared man in the world reduced to a body that had lain helpless for hours while frightened subordinates dithered over whether to summon a doctor. On March 5, 1953, Joseph Stalin died of a cerebral hemorrhage suffered four days earlier — the longest-serving leader of the Soviet Union, his grip on power broken at last by the one authority no decree could overrule. For a generation he had embodied the state so completely that the state hardly knew how to go on without him. The date that took him had, two decades before, handed another tyranny its opening.

On March 5, 1933, Adolf Hitler's Nazi Party drew 43.9 percent at the Reichstag elections. It was not yet a majority, not yet absolute, but it was enough — enough to clear the road to the Enabling Act and the dictatorship that followed. One March 5 watched power consolidate; another watched it dissolve. Twenty years apart, on the same square of the calendar, one tyranny was tightening its fist and the other was finally, in death, loosening it. The almanac offers no moral. It simply sets the seizing and the releasing side by side and lets the symmetry do its quiet work.

The deeper argument of the date runs through the people who dreamed of something authority could not contain. Rosa Luxemburg was born on March 5, 1871 — the revolutionary who insisted that a socialism worth having could not be built on dictatorship, that freedom was always the freedom of the one who thinks differently. She did not live to see either of the men this date would later mark; she was murdered long before, in 1919. But the two regimes that bracket March 5 would each, in their way, betray or crush exactly what she stood for. Her birthday sits between them like a reproach neither could answer.

And then, reaching further back, March 5, 1616: the Catholic Church placed Nicolaus Copernicus's On the Revolutions of the Heavenly Spheres on the Index of Forbidden Books — seventy-three years after it was printed, and far too late to recall the idea it carried. Authority can ban a book. It cannot un-ring the thought. The same instinct that suppressed a heliocentric heaven would echo, centuries on, in regimes that tried to forbid whole ways of seeing the world.

The date is not all weight. March 5, 1898 gave the world Zhou Enlai, first premier of the People's Republic. March 5, 1981 saw Sinclair Research launch the ZX81, a small British machine that put computing into more than a million homes. And March 5, 1966 holds a darker entry — BOAC Flight 911 breaking apart over Mount Fuji in clear-air turbulence, all 124 aboard lost to a force no authority commands. Power grasps, and power slips, and somewhere between the ballot and the dacha floor an old truth keeps surfacing: what can be seized can be lost, and what can be forbidden cannot be unthought.
